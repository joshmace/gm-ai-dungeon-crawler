# Running the Three Knots Pack

This is a B/X-flavored old-school pack. Tone is atmospheric rather than heroic: the dungeon decides, the dice speak, and the characters are not the center of the world's story. They are sellswords, investigators, and explorers — people the village calls when no one else will go.

## Running Style

**Danger is real.** Zero hit points means dead. No death saves, no scene-ending rescue, no plot armor. A careful player who uses torches, retreats when bloodied, and bribes what they cannot kill will live to spend their gold. A reckless one dies in a crypt.

**Treasure is the point.** Gold equals experience. Loot recovered from the Old Empire's ruins advances the character as surely as defeating monsters — often more so. Let the weight of every coin carried back to town register.

**The dungeon has a mood.** Three Knots is a haunting, not a battle map. Describe temperature, air, silence. The Dead King's presence should weigh on every room long before he appears on the throne.

## Signature Mechanics

**Morale rolls.** When a monster is reduced below half its numbers or its leader falls, roll 2d6: 7 or less, the survivors flee or surrender. Use this liberally. The goblins are cowards; the undead, mindless; the Dead King, immovable. Let morale shape combat.

**Reaction rolls.** Not every encounter is a fight. When the attitude of an NPC or creature is uncertain, roll 2d6: 2 hostile, 3–5 unfriendly, 6–8 neutral, 9–11 friendly, 12 helpful. A hungry wolf may be chased off with a torch; a skeleton in a tomb, probably not.

**X-in-6.** Some tasks resolve on a d6 rather than a d20 — thief skills, surprise checks, secret-door detection. Use when the odds are narrow and a d20 swing would be too wide.

**Roll-under resolution.** Checks and saves roll d20; succeed on a roll ≤ the target (the ability score for a raw ability check, the authored value for a categorical save). A natural 1 always succeeds; a natural 20 always fails.

**No advantage/disadvantage.** Situational edges become flat target adjustments instead. A higher position, a good plan, a torch in a creature's face — each is worth a step on the difficulty scale (+2 to the target for an easier check, −2 for a harder one).

**Side-based initiative.** At combat start, one side acts first for the whole round. Use a 2d6 reaction-style roll to decide if it matters; default to the player side when surprise does not figure. Individual initiative is a v2 feature.

## What's Not Here

- **No skills.** When a check is needed, call for the ability directly: "Roll Dexterity." Common tasks auto-succeed; impossible tasks auto-fail. The GM holds the line between them.
- **No damage types.** Fire, cold, and bone-breaking cudgels all do damage; whether they do anything *special* is GM call. The schema does not let us declare a resistance, so do not lean on them.
- **No proficiency bonus.** B/X did not have one. Level 3 hits harder than level 1 because the dice and HP are better, not because the sheet says so.
- **No cantrips, sorcerer points, or power budgets.** The Cleric spellcasts from level 2; the Magic-User starts at level 1 with exactly one spell per day. Thief skills resolve on a d6. Everything else is d20 and hope.

## Tips for Solo Play

- **Torches matter.** Dungeons are dark. A torch burns for an hour, then goes out. Track it; let it shape decisions.
- **Retreat is a strategy.** The character can leave. There is often no penalty for leaving — except, of course, what is still waiting in the tomb.
- **The GM reads the stats.** When the bestiary says HP 8 AC 14, those are the numbers. The GM does not fudge them to make fights survivable. If the dice say the skeleton kills Valen, the skeleton kills Valen. That is the genre.
