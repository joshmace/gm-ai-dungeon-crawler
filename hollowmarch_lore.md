# The Hollowmarch

## World Overview

The Hollowmarch is a long northern strip of country — a month's walk end to end — bounded on one side by the grey crags of the **Spurs** and on the other by the salt-grass plains of the **Lessening**. A hundred years ago it was the northernmost province of the Dawnlight Empire: a working border, well-patrolled, its roads lit from watch-house to watch-house by Warden lanterns that did not go out in any weather. Then the Empire, for reasons still argued over in every inn, broke. The roads un-lit. The watch-houses emptied one by one.

Today the Hollowmarch is what remains: a loose archipelago of small villages and market towns connected by roads that still mostly go where their old maps say they go, with large stretches of un-watched country between them. The villages are quiet and well-defended. The ruins — and there are many — are not, though some of them remember being.

The feel of the place is October light. Harvest smells. Long walks between inns. A lantern in every window that stays lit at nightfall, whether anyone is at home or not.

## Cosmology

Two principles, not much else: the **Light** and the **Undernight**.

The Light is not a god. It is a quality — warmth, memory, making-visible — that the old Wardens taught was the proper condition of the world. Lanterns, hearths, clear speech, buried dead who stay buried. It has saints, after a fashion, and a handful of small orders that still keep vigils in its name.

The Undernight is not a god either, but it is closer to being one. It is forgetting, cold, the unspecific pull of becoming-something-else. The Wardens held the line against it by doing very ordinary work: lighting lamps, naming things, returning the fallen to their graves under their proper names. When the Watch broke, the line did not so much collapse as thin. Out in the country, after dark, it is thin indeed.

Clerics of the Light are rare and usually village-bound; most were taught by a predecessor rather than in any school. They know practical things — how to still a wound, how to close an unquiet grave, how to bless a lantern — and they freely admit that anything grander is beyond them.

## Major Regions

**The Inner Marches** — the settled country around the old capital-of-the-province, **Keep's Cross**. Largest towns, busiest market days, the only standing imperial road that is still repaired. Life here is almost normal. The dark is kept mostly at arm's length.

**The Hollow Edge** — the thinning strip of villages along the Spurs' foothills. Smaller places, more haunted, more stories. Most of the old Warden watch-houses are along the Edge. Most of the ruins adventurers care about are an hour's walk off the road from somewhere on the Edge.

**The Lessening** — the salt-grass plains to the west. Barely populated, mostly because the soil is bad and the weather is worse. Honest folk live there, too few to be worth discussing. The Empire built nothing in the Lessening that was worth leaving behind.

**The Spurs** — the cold limestone mountains to the east. Old dwarven country. The great holds are empty now, their gates walled and sealed from the inside before the last holders left, and no one knows where they went. A few small dwarven families still work limestone quarries in the foothills and come down for market.

## Peoples and Cultures

**Humans** are most of everyone. The folk of the Hollowmarch are a mix of pre-imperial stock (quieter, longer-memoried, keep old customs in the backs of their heads) and Dawnlight settler descendants (practical, literate, good at trades). Villages are small enough that the distinction doesn't matter most days.

**Dwarves** are remnants. The big holds under the Spurs — **Morrowdeep**, **Iron Cradle**, **the Long Hall at Karnak** — were sealed from the inside about sixty years ago. The few dwarves left in the Hollowmarch are limestone-quarrymen in the foothills: small family operations, conservative, polite, reluctant to speak of what's behind the great gates. Their old craftwork shows up in village attics and ruined watch-houses. Lantern-work owes them a great deal.

**Fey** are not a people but a rumor. Deep in the Spurs, on certain nights, in certain stands of birch. Adventurers may encounter something that looks like a young woman with too-long fingers or a fox with a very small hat. Villagers do not ask. The Wardens used to know how to deal with them. That knowledge is mostly lost.

**Goblinkind** are the complication. Long before the Empire, a forgotten cult bred goblins and hobgoblins as warrior-stock; when the cult ended (nobody is quite clear how) the goblinkind scattered and went feral across the northern country. They have clans, customs, grudges, and enough cleverness to run a raid. Hobgoblins in particular still remember being soldiers and still organize like it. Dawnlight fought wars with them. The Hollowmarch has brief ones every generation.

There are **no elves**, **no orcs**, **no halflings** in the Hollowmarch as the term is usually understood. The country is human, dwarven-remnant, goblin-frontier, and fey-rumor. Travellers from elsewhere do exist — in Keep's Cross, in the rarer inns — but they are always travellers.

## History

**Before the Empire** (~1000+ years ago): the pre-imperial cults built barrows, standing stones, and a great deal of grim business in hidden places. Most of the adventure-worthy ruins in the Hollowmarch predate the Empire by several centuries. Many of them contain something the Empire thought it had dealt with.

**The Dawnlight Empire** (~800 years ago to ~100 years ago): a human civilization centered to the south. Founded the **Wardens of the Watch** as a hybrid order — part border-militia, part priest-caste of the Light, part craftwork guild — to hold its new northern borders against the dark the pre-imperial cults had loosed. The Wardens were competent, uncharismatic, and effective. Under them, the Hollowmarch was a boring place to live, which was widely considered an improvement.

**The Break** (~100 years ago): the Empire collapsed. The cause is argued over. Some say a plague. Some say a succession crisis. Some — the ones whose families have been in the Hollowmarch longest — say something came out of the Inner Empire that nobody talks about now. Whichever it was, the southern administration went silent, the roads stopped being patrolled from both ends, and the Wardens of the Watch were asked to hold on their own.

**The Long Vigil and the Breaking of the Watch** (~40 years after the Break): they held, remarkably, for forty years. Without imperial pay, without resupply, without replacement, the Wardens kept the lanterns lit and the barrows closed. Then, in a year now called **the Falling Winter**, most of the Watch was lost in a single campaign against something coming out of **the Blackfall** (a barrow-complex in the Spurs that had always been trouble). The survivors held on for another few years and then, one by one, put down their lanterns. There are no Wardens now, though every generation there is a child who tries to build one.

**The Present** (the last 60 years): village self-rule, light trade along the old imperial road, slow loss of old lantern-work to theft and decay, and a very slow reassertion of the dark. Villages know most of what's near them. Ruins two hours into the woods are usually someone else's problem.

## Magic

**Lantern-work** is the defining craft of the Hollowmarch, and the reason adventurers bother coming here. It was a Warden tradition — a hybrid of dwarven metallurgy, Light-clerical blessing, and a specific meditative craft practice — that produced objects that carried small, patient magics. A **Lanternblade** sheds light and will not be put out. A **wardstone** holds a threshold closed against the undead. A **hearth-lantern** keeps an inn warm through a whole winter on one bead of oil. No one makes new lantern-work now. All of it is found.

**Village magic** is hedge-wisdom: midwives, herbwives, the woman-in-the-small-house-at-the-edge-of-town. Real but small. It treats fevers and delivers babies and, on a good night, can talk a troubled sleep out of a child.

**Clerical magic of the Light** is rare but present. A village priest, if the village has one, can close wounds and re-bless a lantern. A senior cleric (there are perhaps a dozen in the Hollowmarch at any time) can perform the proper laying-to-rest of the dead, which matters more here than in most places.

**Arcane scholarship** exists but is imported: traveling wizards from the surviving southern cities, occasional scholars from the coast. They are regarded with polite suspicion. Local wizards are rare and usually self-taught from inherited books.

**Necromancy** — the magic of the Undernight — is what the Wardens were built to hold back, and what is slowly waking along the Hollow Edge. It is native to the Hollowmarch's pre-imperial past. Practitioners are exceedingly rare and invariably bad news.

## Adventure Context

The Hollowmarch is dungeon-friendly country. Ruins are many and various, most are unclaimed, and villages near any of them will usually pay (or feed, or point in the direction of another village that will pay) for the clearing of a nearby trouble-spot. The standard shape of a Hollowmarch adventure is:

1. Arrive in a village.
2. Learn of a local problem — a barrow that has begun to hum; a watch-house whose lanterns relit themselves; a crypt whose seal broke; a traveller who never came back from a particular stretch of road.
3. Go to the ruin.
4. Clear, investigate, or return the thing to its proper state.
5. Come back for a meal and a mattress and, possibly, a reward.

A one-shot is plausibly a single barrow or a single watch-house. A short campaign is a tour of three or four along the Hollow Edge. A long campaign goes toward **the Blackfall**, the barrow-complex where the Watch was broken. No one who plans a long campaign has come back from the Blackfall.

**What adventurers typically find:**
- Old lantern-work worth hauling out
- Warden artifacts — trail maps, codebooks, the occasional sealed chest with a proper Warden's kit intact
- Pre-imperial treasure — gold, gems, older dwarven metalwork
- Various undead, in various states of awake-ness
- Goblin raiding parties that have claimed a ruin for their own
- Occasionally, much worse

## GM Guidance

**Pace slow.** Villages are a day or two apart. Ruins are an hour or two off the road. Most sessions should start and end at an inn.

**The dark is the antagonist.** Not a single villain. The Undernight is patient, diffuse, and present wherever lanterns are not. Fights with undead are not about beating bad guys — they are about returning something to a state of being-put-away. Let the players feel that difference in narration.

**The Wardens are dead, but still shape the world.** Honor their memory in how you describe their old works: competent, plain, durable, unpretentious. Their ruins are not grand. Their uniforms, where you find fragments, are grey and dark blue. Their marks are small stylized lanterns. Players who learn about the Watch should come to respect it. Players who bear Warden gear (a Lanternblade, a wardstone, a rubbing) may find villagers treat them differently — with cautious hope, mostly.

**Villages are safe.** This is important. The Hollowmarch is cozy-horror, not grimdark. A village is a place the players can come back to. Villages have names, innkeepers, regulars, smells. A session that ends at an inn — warm food, a known bed, a conversation with the lamplighter — is a good session.

**Dwarves are not playable in this setting, but are allies.** A dwarven NPC in a village is a quiet figure with a dry sense of humor, skilled, reluctant to discuss his people's holds, and usually willing to repair gear for a reasonable fee. Treat them with respect; they've been through the same thing as the humans, only harder.

**Goblins are not comic relief.** They are a frontier opponent — clever, organized at small scale, cruel at larger scale. Hobgoblins in particular are disciplined and should be narrated as the soldiers they once were. A hobgoblin legionary is not a joke.

**If a ruin has a name, villagers know it.** If a character asks, tell them. Withhold only the specific horrors inside, not the reputation. The Hollowmarch is well-rumored country.

**Lanterns matter.** When the party enters a dark place, mention the lantern. When the lantern flickers, mention that too. A dropped torch in a crypt is a scene. A lanternless party deep underground is in trouble whether or not the GM says so out loud.
