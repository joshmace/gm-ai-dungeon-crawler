# The Haunting of Three Knots — GM Running Notes

## The heart of the module

Three weeks ago, four treasure-seekers broke the seal on the king's tomb north of the village. One came back, and he did not come back right, and he is buried now. The village has not slept properly since. The haunting is not a metaphor — it is a real bleed from a real place — but the module is not a mystery. The player knows where the source is. The question is whether they can walk down there and stand up when they come out.

Honor that in the narration. The tomb is not a puzzle-box. It is a straight line, and the Dead King is waiting at the end of it.

## Pacing — 60 to 90 minutes

- 10 min: the inn. Mara, Corbin, the commission. Set the village in the player's mind as something worth saving.
- 5 min: the tomb road and the broken seal. Exposition beat — somebody who knew the seal cut through it.
- 10 min: antechapel + corridor skeleton. First combat. Tone-setter.
- 15 min: the chamber of the seal. Puzzle room. Let the player sit with it.
- 10 min: the hall of blades. Trap room. Let the player *look* before they step.
- 10 min: antechamber + tomb guardian. Second combat. Noticeably harder than the skeleton.
- 15–25 min: the throne room. The Dead King. The climax.
- 5 min: return to the inn. The mayor's reward.

A faster run is fine. A slower one is better than a rushed one; the tomb's mood is half the module.

## The Dead King — combat calibration

This is a B/X-flavored module. The fight at the throne is genuinely dangerous for a level-1 fighter solo, and it is *meant* to be. The player is not given the Sword of the Dawn before the fight on purpose — the reward is the reward for surviving the fight, not a tool for winning it. If the player asks why Corbin won't give them the sword up front, Corbin says: "The village keeps it for a proper need. The need is proving you'll come back." Let him be a little unkind about it.

Tuning notes:

- **Drain of the Crown** is the Dead King's closer, not his opener. Save it for a clearly-wounded player. When it lands, the max-HP reduction is real and only a full night's rest in a warm place (the inn — not the tomb, not the road) clears it.
- **Dawn Vulnerability** is a signpost, not a gotcha. A player carrying a blessed weapon, the occasional quest-gift from a prior module, or a cleric's equivalent gets the extra 1d6. Most players will not have it. That is fine; the fight is built to be winnable without it.
- **No morale checks.** The Dead King does not flee. Narrate the pale fire in his eyes dimming as his HP falls, so the player knows the end is within reach.
- **Escape beats.** If the fight is going wrong and the player has not had a chance to breathe, the Dead King may break off for a round after a clean hit from the player — he has eternity, and he can afford to savor this. Use sparingly.

If the player dies in the tomb, they die in the tomb. Three Knots does not soften that. The village is lost with them; the coda is a single paragraph of wind in the hills.

## The puzzle — crown, sceptre, coffin

One solution. The three pillars are pressed in the order of a king's life: *reign, rule, rest*. Crown first (the throne is taken), sceptre second (the rod is held), coffin last (the king lies down). A wrong order resets the pillars with a harmless flash; there is no failure penalty except time. If the player is stuck for more than a couple of minutes, offer the INT check as a fallback, and read the success narration.

Do not drop additional hints beyond the lintel inscription and the carvings on the pillars themselves. The puzzle is not difficult; the player should have the satisfaction of arriving at it.

## The hall of blades — look before you step

The trap is detect-then-avoid. A careful player reads the floor, spots the raised flags, and crosses without rolling. That is the intended path; the 25 XP detection reward is there to make "I stop at the threshold and look" the correct move, every time. The avoidance roll is for players who walk in without looking; the damage (1d6+1, average 4) is meant to hurt but not end a fighter. A player who fails the avoidance roll is through the hall — the hazard does not re-fire within a single crossing.

On a return trip through the hall (retreating to the inn, for instance), the hazard does not re-fire; the player already knows the safe path.

## Mara and Corbin — NPC voice

**Mara Thornwood.** Plain, broad, grey-braided, not given to drama. Speaks in short sentences. She is relieved to see someone who looks capable of carrying a sword. She will not say so in as many words; the village is frightened and she will not make it look more frightened than it is. If asked directly what is in the tomb, she says she does not know, because she does not.

**Mayor Corbin.** Spare, grey, formal. Was a soldier once — a small county levy, nothing grand — and the discipline is still in him. He does not stand when the player comes over; this is not rudeness, it is that his knee is bad and the village knows it. He commissions the player without embellishment: the village needs the tomb closed, the purse is one hundred gold, the blade is the Sword of the Dawn, and the village will pay the debt in full on return. He means every word.

The delivery of the reward on return should be the warmest scene in the module. Corbin stands this time. The village crowds the common room. The sword in the hall sheds its faint clean light, and somebody at the back draws breath audibly. This is the payoff for a player who came back.

## Rules-pack-specific notes

- **Morale.** The corridor skeleton, the tomb guardian, and the Dead King are all mindless and do not check morale. Use morale rolls anyway in scenes where the player outwits a non-undead foe in the coda or a sequel (bandits, goblins, wolves) — the rules pack carries morale because the wider world uses it.
- **Reaction rolls.** There is no uncertain reaction in this module; Mara is friendly, Corbin is neutral-shading-formal, the tomb is hostile. Reserve the reaction roll for sequels.
- **Side-based initiative.** The three combats are simple enough that default-to-player-side is fine. Roll for initiative only if a second instance of a monster is added (a sequel, a house-rule).
- **No advantage/disadvantage.** A higher position, a good plan, or a torch in a creature's face is worth a step on the DC scale (−2) rather than a second die. The hall of blades is the only check where this really matters; let the player's prep earn them the easy DC.
- **Typeless damage.** The rules pack does not carry damage types. The Dead King's Drain of the Crown is mechanically typeless — it is the max-HP reduction on save-fail that makes it feel different, not a "necrotic" tag. Read the save aloud, narrate the cold, let the player feel it.

## Things to withhold, things to reveal

**Withhold:** the order of the pillars. The specific mechanic of Drain of the Crown (the player should feel the reduction as a wound that will not close rather than read it as a number on a sheet). That the Dead King cannot be turned, talked to, or negotiated with — let the player find that out.

**Reveal freely:** that the haunting began when the tomb was disturbed. That the seal on the inner door is a ward of light, not a lock. That the hall of blades is a trap — the darts have been firing for centuries and the bones between the flags are plain to see. That the mayor's reward is the Sword of the Dawn; the player is owed that detail so the reward at the end is a promise kept rather than a surprise.
