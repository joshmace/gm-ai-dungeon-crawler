# World Overview

The Shattered Realms are a world in recovery. Two centuries ago the Sundering — a single cataclysmic day — emptied the cities of the Old Empire and left less than one person in ten alive. The great roads became overgrown; the great magics failed; the dead, once quiet, began to rise.

This is the Age of Reclamation. Kingdoms are rebuilding. Trade has returned to some routes. But the ruins remain, heavy with treasure and haunted by what the Sundering left behind. Adventurers go where armies will not: into crypts and broken towers, to end small hauntings before they grow, to recover what was lost, and, occasionally, to learn what caused it all.

The tone is dark but hopeful. Survival is possible. Heroism is possible. Permanence — of people, of places, of what you build — is not.

# Cosmology

The mortal world sits at the boundary between realms that once were more distinct. Four planes are still spoken of:

- **The Material Plane** — where the living live.
- **The Shadowfell** — the realm of death and silence. Since the Sundering it bleeds through old tombs and dead places more readily than it should.
- **The Feywild** — realm of chaotic magic. Distant now, its old paths grown cold.
- **The Astral** — thought and divine power. Rarely accessed without calamity.

The elemental realms exist but have been largely unreachable since the Sundering collapsed the great binding rites.

## Gods worshipped in the Age of Reclamation

**The Keeper** — god of death, graves, and memory. Symbol: a lantern with no flame. The Keeper's priests tend the dead and work to lay restless spirits. After the Sundering, their work has never been more needed.

**The Lifegiver** — god of growth and healing. Symbol: a tree with golden leaves. Patron of midwives, healers, and farmers.

**The Wanderer** — god of travel and trade. Symbol: a road disappearing over the horizon. Protects merchants and travelers; strictly neutral.

**The Forge** — god of crafting, civilization, and law. Symbol: hammer and anvil. Patron of builders, smiths, and those trying to put things back together.

Souls journey to the Keeper's realm unless bound by magic or unfinished business. An unbound soul is not considered cursed; a bound one almost always is.

# Major Regions

**The Sundered Coast** — where the Sundering began. Still magically unstable. Mostly ruins and scavengers; the anomalies have not settled.

**The Kingdom of Aldwyn** — the largest surviving human kingdom, rebuilding under a feudal monarchy. Most adventures start here; its borderlands are where the old horrors still pour out.

**The Old Empire** — the ruins of the pre-Sundering civilization. Nothing lives there now. The loot is good; the price is proportional.

**The Wildwood** — the great forest, home to the reclusive elves. They were scarred by the Sundering worst of all, and regard outsiders with suspicion.

# Peoples and Cultures

**Humans** — the most common, the most adaptable, the most ambitious. They have made the hardest choices to survive.

**Dwarves** — survived the Sundering underground. Now re-emerging into a world they do not fully recognize. Clan-based, long-lived, pragmatic.

**Elves** — rare, reclusive, and deeply distrustful. The Sundering cost them worst of all. The old elven cities are gone.

**Halflings** — common in human lands. Nomadic traders and farmers; well-adapted to the new world.

# History

## The Age of Wonders (ended 200 years ago)

The Old Empire stood for centuries — a continental civilization with great cities, powerful magic, and long peace. It is remembered more clearly than any age since, because everyone studies its ruins.

## The Sundering (Day of Ash, 200 years ago)

One day, the world broke. The cause is still debated; theories range from an archmage's experiment to divine punishment to a war between powers no one now living can name. Less than ten percent of the population survived. The magic that did not fail outright became dangerous. The dead did not rest.

## The Dark Years (first century after the Sundering)

Survival, scattered communities, constant danger. Undead hordes. Starvation. Disease. Heroes in the small sense — people who kept others alive through the worst of it.

## The Reclamation (current century)

Rebuilding, reclaiming land, rediscovering what was lost. New kingdoms. Restored trade routes. A growing population, at last. Still: many ruins unexplored, undead still rising, magic still unreliable.

# Magic

Magic is real but dangerous and unpredictable since the Sundering. The great magical schools are gone; most practitioners are self-taught or work from fragments. Spells sometimes backfire. Scrolls sometimes misfire. The old rites sometimes do what they should, and sometimes do something else entirely.

The Sundering weakened the barrier between life and death, which is why the dead rise so readily. Most of what haunts the Realms is not monsters in the traditional sense — it is consequences.

Artifacts of the Old Empire remain buried in tombs and ruins. They are rare, powerful, and worth the journey. They are also, often, cursed.

# Adventure Context

Adventurers — those willing to go where others will not — do well in the Reclamation. Kingdoms pay bounties on undead. Merchants pay for ruin-cleared trade routes. Scholars pay for fragments of Old Empire writing. Villages pay, when they can, for a hero to end a haunting.

**Common threats:**

- Undead of all kinds (by far the most common)
- Bandits and raiders
- Magical anomalies — zones where the Sundering still acts
- Monsters that crossed over during the Sundering and never found their way home
- Cultists seeking to repeat the Sundering, for reasons of their own
- Rival adventuring parties — everyone is after the same ruins

**Common opportunities:** treasure from Old Empire ruins; bounties; lost knowledge; new land to settle; glory.

# GM Guidance

**Tone.** Dark but hopeful. The world is dangerous but worth saving. Small victories matter.

**Motivation.** Gold, glory, and a safer world. Most PCs are mercenary but not without conscience.

**Moral ambiguity.** Not everything is black and white. Survivors of the Dark Years made hard choices. Some of them still walk around.

**Mystery.** The Sundering's cause is the great question. Drop clues. Let players wonder. Do not resolve it cheaply.

**Death.** Common but meaningful. A PC who dies should be mourned; their story should leave tracks.

**Magic items.** Old Empire artifacts are special. Finding one is a moment. Do not hand them out lightly, and do not strip them of history when you do.

**Undead.** Treat them with horror, not just XP. They are not villains by choice — they are consequences of the Sundering. The old king whose tomb you are clearing was a person once. This is a tragedy as much as a monster fight.
