# Valen Cross — Character Notes

## Appearance

Late twenties. Tall and plain. Dark-haired, close-cropped the way a man cuts his own hair with a knife by the fire. Eyes grey and steady. The face is neither handsome nor not — it is the face of a man who has been in a number of rooms and been paid attention to in none of them. He wears his chain mail as if it were an undershirt: not proudly, not reluctantly; simply because in the work he does, one wears it. The longsword across his hip is older than his career.

Sits with his back to the wall, without making a show of it. Places his cup carefully, not for ceremony — so he knows where it is.

## Personality

Quiet. Watchful. Does not volunteer. Answers directly when asked, in short plain sentences, and does not embroider. Courteous with strangers in a way that is not warmth — it is the care of a man who has seen what happens in a room when courtesy fails.

Valen is a sellsword and does not pretend otherwise. He works for his rate, takes the contract he is offered, and does what the contract says. The contract at Three Knots was framed to him as *close the tomb*; that is what he came for. If the village turns out to need more than that, he will do more than that, because he does not like leaving work half-done and because Mara Thornwood made him feel, over a single evening's meal, that the village's trouble is not a job the village deserved.

He does not speechify. He does not make jokes when steel is drawn. He will not tell you his story unless you ask directly, and even then the answers are short.

## Backstory

Valen was born in **Oakstand**, a small grain town on the Inner Marches three days south of the Shattered Crown. Middle child of a cooper and a weaver. He was not the kind of boy who ran toward trouble, and he was not the kind of boy who ran away from it either — he watched it, and measured it, and by thirteen he was the one in a scuffle who stepped in last and ended it.

At seventeen he took the county levy for a season against a bandit-crew working the Marches road, and discovered he was competent at the work and that the work, after the first engagement, did not trouble him at night. He took a contract with a traveling merchant-guard company, then another, then a small free-company under a captain named **Orren Sallow**. Sallow died in his sleep of a bad heart three winters ago; the company dissolved; Valen has been working alone since.

He has no living family he writes to. There is a woman in a village a week's ride east named **Iona Bell** who he does not write to either, and about whom he does not speak, and whose name is in a letter folded into the back of his belt he has never sent. The letter is from him. He has not decided what it says.

He arrived in Three Knots looking for a week's work mucking stables or cutting firewood, found the village in the state it was in, and stayed.

## Class and play notes

**Fighter, B/X.** No tricks, no action economy buttons, no per-rest reserves. At level 3 Valen has no mechanical class features to spend — only better hit dice, a steadier arm, and the weight of 4,000 XP between him and a first-level greenhorn. That is the point of the B/X fighter: the sword is the mechanic.

**Hit points.** 19 at full. A single bad round with the Dead King can drop him below half; two bad rounds can end him. Play patient, use cover, retreat when bloodied.

**Saves.** Categorical B/X. Death 12, Wands 13, Paralysis 14, Breath 15, Spells 16 — roll the listed number or higher on a d20. Valen's best save is against death itself, which is appropriate; his worst is against spells, which is also appropriate.

**Equipment.** Chain mail, iron-rimmed shield, longsword. Shortbow in the pack for the rare range shot; a lantern and three torches for the tomb; two healing potions that are more than most mercenaries his age carry, because he is cautious. No silvered weapon; the silver dagger is a purchase he has been meaning to make and has not.

**What he does not have.** The Sword of the Dawn. That is the reward, and it is meant to be the reward. Do not let the player leverage Corbin into handing it over early; Corbin will not.

## Voice in play

Short declarative sentences. Names his intent, then acts. "I'll take the corridor first." "I don't like the feel of this floor." "Step back. I'll draw it off."

When addressed in the second person by the GM narration, Valen responds with a nod more often than words. He thanks Mara by nodding and paying in coin rather than by speaking. He addresses Corbin as *sir* because Corbin was a soldier; he does not address Mara as *ma'am* because Mara would laugh.

## Things Valen cares about, in order

1. Finishing work he has been paid to do.
2. Not spending what he does not have — coins, oil, bowstrings, attention.
3. A dry bed and a warm meal, in that order.
4. The letter in the back of his belt.
5. Iona Bell.

## Things Valen is uneasy about

- Tombs.
- Talk of what a man owes the dead.
- Being spoken about in a room he is sitting in.
- The way the Sword of the Dawn is spoken of in the village — as if the village already knows it has to be given away.
